# Calibration — do our per-circuit neutralisation odds actually predict?

The strategy simulator prices every lap with a per-circuit probability of a
Full Course Yellow or Safety Car. This report holds those numbers to the only
standard that matters for a forecast: **out-of-sample skill**. Each race
edition is left out, its probability is formed from the *other* editions of
the same circuit (Jeffreys `(k+0.5)/(n+1)`), and the prediction is graded with
proper scoring rules. `src/prediction/`, regenerated by
`scripts/run_prediction_backtest.py`.

The **Brier skill score** is the headline: the fractional improvement in Brier
score over predicting the base rate every time. Positive means the grouping
genuinely forecasts; zero means it adds nothing over the long-run average;
negative means it is *worse* than knowing nothing.

| Target | Level | Races | Base rate | Brier | Brier (base rate) | Skill | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| IMSA FCY | circuit | 63 | 0.97 | 0.046 | 0.031 | -0.505 | no better than base rate |
| WEC FCY | circuit | 33 | 0.73 | 0.217 | 0.198 | -0.095 | no better than base rate |
| WEC Safety Car | circuit | 33 | 0.70 | 0.244 | 0.211 | -0.158 | no better than base rate |
| F1 Safety Car | circuit | 27 | 0.52 | 0.320 | 0.250 | -0.281 | no better than base rate |
| F1 VSC | circuit | 27 | 0.30 | 0.259 | 0.208 | -0.244 | no better than base rate |
| Endurance FCY (by series) | series | 96 | 0.89 | 0.093 | 0.102 | +0.081 | beats base rate |

## What this says (and it is not the flattering answer)

**Per circuit, the neutralisation odds do not beat the base rate.** At 6-8 F1
editions and 3-11 endurance editions per circuit, the leave-one-out per-circuit
frequency is dominated by sampling noise: its skill is around zero or negative
for every target. Knowing *which* circuit you are at does not, on this evidence,
let you out-predict the series-wide rate of a Safety Car.

**The harness is not rigged to fail, though — it finds signal where signal
exists.** The positive control (endurance FCY grouped by *series*) has clearly
positive skill, because IMSA (~0.97 FCY) and WEC (~0.73) genuinely differ. A
backtest that only ever returned zero would be untestable; this one
distinguishes a real effect (series) from noise (circuit), which is the whole
point of scoring a prediction instead of admiring a fit.

**So this is a limitation the project states about its own simulator, with a
number attached.** The per-circuit FCY/SC probabilities are still the best
available inputs and their posteriors already carry wide credible intervals
(the safety-car reports say so); what this backtest adds is the proof that the
*point estimates* should be read as little more than the series rate until many
more editions accumulate. The rolling pipeline keeps ingesting them, and this
report is regenerated each time — so the day a circuit does earn measurable
skill, the table will show it.

## Method notes

- **Proper scoring rules only.** Brier and log-loss are minimised only by
  reporting your true belief, so the score cannot be gamed by hedging.
- **Leave-one-race-out.** A race never contributes to its own prediction, so
  the skill is genuinely out-of-sample, not an in-sample fit.
- **Jeffreys smoother** `(k+0.5)/(n+1)` keeps a circuit seen once at an
  uninformative 0.5 rather than a falsely-certain 0 or 1.
- **F1 outcomes** are reconstructed from the committed per-circuit occurrence
  counts (`data/derived/f1/sc_model.csv`); the leave-one-out prediction depends
  only on the event count `k` and edition count `n`, never on which season, so
  the counts are sufficient. Endurance outcomes come per-race from the lap-level
  flag table (`data/derived/endurance/race_flags.csv`).
- **IMSA Safety Car is omitted**: there is none in the data, so there is nothing
  to predict (see the IMSA simulator report).
